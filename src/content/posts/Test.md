The most forgettable products are usually functional enough.

They load. They save data. They respond to clicks. They satisfy the checklist that proves a team shipped something.

What they do not do is create confidence.

Confidence is the feeling that the system has been thought through. It comes from details that seem small in isolation: naming, spacing, defaults, interaction timing, and whether a confusing moment has been designed away before it ever reaches the user.

I keep coming back to the idea that aesthetics are part of engineering. Not because everything needs polish theater, but because visual and interaction decisions communicate system quality long before a user has language for it.

If a product wants to feel intelligent, it should first feel composed.
